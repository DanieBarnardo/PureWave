# The Cave: DIY Home Theatre on a Budget

For most of the past decade, my home cinema has grown the slow way.

Not with one massive credit-card swipe. Not with a dream-spec equipment list ordered in a weekend. It has grown through bargain hunting, careful upgrades, DIY experiments, and the occasional decision that was easier to explain after the fact than before it.

That is probably the real origin story of The Cave.

Like many home theatre enthusiasts with families, budgets, and real houses to work around, I did not start with a blank slate. I started with constraints. Some were practical. Some were financial. Some had to do with doors, throw distance, and inconvenient walls. And some had to do with the timeless negotiation between "living room" and "dedicated cinema."

Still, over time, the room became something special: a 7.3.4 Dolby Atmos home theatre built piece by piece, with a 100-inch fixed-frame screen, an Epson EH-TW9300 projector, and a collection of carefully chosen components that perform far beyond what the budget might suggest.

What makes the room satisfying is not that everything is textbook. It is that every upgrade was aimed at a better movie night, better music, and fewer compromises that get in the way of immersion.

## 2025: The Year Things Escalated

Looking back, 2025 feels like one of those years that somehow contained three years' worth of projects.

Work kept me busy, but the creative itch never really goes away. So the room kept evolving. The two biggest changes were a monster DIY subwoofer build and a major screen upgrade. Around those headline projects, a bunch of smaller ideas also came to life: fresh-air automation, occupancy sensing, lighting logic, and quieter power amplification for the subs.

The result was a room that moved a long way from "assembled system" toward something far more custom, more intelligent, and more immersive.

## The 13Hz Subwoofer Build

The brief was simple enough: build a subwoofer for around R4000 that could play below 20Hz.

As usual, things escalated.

The finished sub came in at around 50kg, used a 15-inch driver, played down to roughly 13Hz, and landed at a little over R3500. In other words, it overshot the original goal in exactly the right way.

That project changed the room. Low-frequency performance finally reached the point where the system felt grounded. Bass stopped being something you merely heard and became something you felt in the seat, in the floor, and in the atmosphere of the room.

For a budget-led DIY build, that kind of extension is deeply satisfying. Not because of the spec on paper, but because it shifts the emotional scale of a film. The room starts to breathe differently.

## When the Klipsch Plate Amp Died

Just before the holidays, the plate amplifier in my 15-inch Klipsch gave up.

That left me with two options:

1. Repair the amplifier.
2. Convert the subwoofer to passive and use an external amplifier.

I looked into repair first, but finding someone who felt genuinely qualified turned into its own side quest. So I went with the second option and converted the Klipsch into a passive sub.

The original Klipsch amp was rated at 400W RMS into 4 ohms, so the hunt was on for something that could at least match that. Preferably with headroom. Definitely at a dirt-cheap price.

Facebook Marketplace did what Facebook Marketplace sometimes does best. I found SMART Cinema TA2050 amplifiers and Altec Lansing 9444B units for around R1500 each. These are proper pro amps, and the SMART in bridged mode can deliver serious power into 4 ohms.

After the conversion, the patient recovered beautifully. The Klipsch came back stronger, with more control and far more shove than before.

## The Silent Amp Upgrade

There was just one problem.

The pro amps sounded great. Their cooling fans did not.

They were loud enough to be a real issue in a cinema room. Not "slightly annoying." More like "small jet preparing for takeoff." So out came the soldering iron again.

I replaced the stock fans with specialised silent units, and the difference was dramatic. Noise dropped from roughly 53dB SPL to around 20dB. That is the kind of change you do not need a graph to appreciate. The room simply feels calmer. Quieter. More expensive than it really is.

It is a good reminder that performance is not just about output. Sometimes the upgrade that matters most is removing the thing that keeps taking you out of the moment.

## Dreaming Bigger: From 100 Inches to 136

Once the sound was in a much better place, the screen started feeling too small.

That sounds ridiculous when you already have a 100-inch projection screen, but home theatre has a way of recalibrating your idea of "enough." At the same time, large televisions were dropping in price, and some were starting to rival projector image size at a level that felt uncomfortably close.

That would not do.

So I did what any sensible enthusiast does when cash is tight and the room is awkward: I opened Blender and started modelling the problem.

The room was recreated to scale. Once I could see it properly in 3D, the answer became obvious. I could not really make the screen larger by pushing it wider into the existing wall constraints. But what I actually wanted was not "more inches" in isolation. I wanted a bigger viewing angle. I wanted the image to feel larger from the seats.

The solution was to bring the screen further into the room.

That kicked off a detailed design exercise involving custom brackets, side panels, exact dimensions, and one very important constraint: it had to look neat enough to survive spousal review. The side panel in particular had to hide the brackets from the nearby sliding-door entrance while still being rigid, removable, and cleanly finished.

A trip to Builders and about R400 worth of materials later, the fabrication started. The brackets were built from scratch. The side panel came together. The installation happened while the house was briefly quiet and the timing felt strategically wise.

With help from my daughter, the old screen came down, the brackets went up, the side panel slotted into place, and the screen was re-hung. The result matched the Blender render almost perfectly.

And then came the next snag.

Moving the screen changed the projector throw relationship. At maximum zoom, there was still unused image area. Which meant, of course, that the system was now telling me the screen could go even bigger. So up into the ceiling I went again, grinder in hand, modifying the projector mount bracket of my own design.

That mount ended up being one of the most satisfying bits of DIY in the room: a fully adjustable bracket built from four 10x50mm threaded rods suspending a rotating projector platform, for less than R200 in materials.

The final result was a 136-inch acoustically transparent 16:9 screen and a major jump in immersion. The cinema experience shifted from "very good" to something much closer to truly enveloping.

## Hitting a Wall

For years, there had been a friendly tug-of-war in our house about what this room actually was.

My wife saw it as a dual-purpose lounge.

I felt that once a projector entered the story, that ship had sailed. At that point it was a cinema.

The wall to the left of the screen carried the scars of that compromise. It was bright. Decorative. Entirely wrong for projection. Great for a lounge. Bad for immersion.

So during the December holiday, I took advantage of a long shopping trip and got to work.

I built a timber frame, wrapped it in black stretch fabric, reused the internals of my left-wall acoustic panel, and mounted the whole thing in place. My daughter assisted, which I would like to think counts as either character-building or legal mitigation.

The effect was immediate.

Yes, the image gained a little more pop. But the bigger win was psychological. The screen now floated in front of a darker, quieter visual field. Distractions fell away. The room felt more intentional. More cinematic. Less like a lounge pretending to be one.

That is often how these changes work. On paper, the gain looks small. In practice, the room feels transformed.

## Small Projects That Made the Room Easier to Live With

Not every meaningful upgrade in 2025 was big and visible.

I also built an automated fresh-air intake controlled by a microprocessor and air-quality sensor. It was surprisingly affordable. The most expensive parts were the soldering kit and the grow-house fan.

Since I already had the soldering iron out, I built a presence sensor using millimetre-wave radar to detect occupancy in the theatre. By monitoring projector and player states, the room lighting became much smarter:

- Foot lights respond automatically when a movie is paused or resumed.
- Main lights react when the projector is off and people enter or leave the room.

It is the kind of upgrade that sounds small until you live with it. Then suddenly the room feels less fiddly and frustrating, and far easier to use.

That matters. A cinema can sound brilliant and still be irritating if the user experience is clumsy.

## Why All of This Matters

None of these projects happened in isolation.

The subwoofer build improved scale and authority. The amplifier conversion added headroom. The silent fan upgrade lowered the room noise floor. The bigger screen improved viewing angle and immersion. The darker wall treatment increased visual focus. The automation upgrades made the room easier and more satisfying to use.

That is the real lesson in The Cave.

High-performance home cinema is not only about buying expensive products. It is about understanding what the room is failing to do, then solving those problems in the right order. Sometimes that means buying. Sometimes that means building. Often it means standing in the room long enough to see that the obvious answer is not the right one.

## The Room Today

What started as a budget-aware hobby project has become a highly personal cinema space shaped by years of iteration.

It is not a showroom. It is not a catalogue room. It is better than that.

It is a room built around the experience itself.

And that is probably why each upgrade has felt worth it. Not because the gear list got longer, but because movie nights got better.

## Closing Thought

If there is one thing this room keeps proving, it is that good home theatre decisions rarely come from hype. They come from patient upgrades, practical problem-solving, and the willingness to experiment.

Measured decisions instead of forum folklore.
